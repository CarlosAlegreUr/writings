# Critique: Chapter 2 — On quitting beliefs: What is intelligence?

## Pacing and Structure

The chapter is called "What is intelligence?" but the actual definition doesn't show up until section 2.5, past the halfway mark. Before that, roughly 80 lines are spent on religion (section 2.3). This is a structural problem. A chapter titled "What is intelligence?" that spends a third of itself on religion before delivering the definition is testing the reader's patience for no good reason. The connection (beliefs, fixed variables, intelligence) exists, but the reader was promised intelligence and got a long religious detour instead.

The **fixed variable resolution technique** is introduced in section 2.1 with just two sentences, then not concretely illustrated until the math examples at the very end (section 2.6). That is too much distance between introducing a key concept and actually showing what it looks like.

**Section 2.3 (Religion)** is the longest section — roughly a third of the chapter. For a chapter about intelligence, this is bloated. The three-benefit framework is clean and the substitutes are practical, but a reader opening a chapter on intelligence did not sign up for an extended analysis of religious psychology. Either the chapter title is wrong or the section is too long. Pick one.

**Section 2.4 (Rationality and faith)** is one of the strongest moments in the chapter. The F=ma analogy is original and clicks — beliefs as part of the "rate of change" rather than opponents of change. This section is brief compared to the religion section, which is the opposite of what it should be. The best idea in the chapter gets less space than the setup material.

**Section 2.5.1 (Are you dumb or intelligent?)** makes the same point about five times with different framings. That's not "circling" — it's repetitive. The professional who is religious, the runner who can't cook, free people acting stupidly — each adds a slightly different angle, but the cumulative effect is a section that restates instead of advances. The runner/cooking example is the strongest. It could do most of the work on its own. Cut the rest.

The **math examples** (section 2.6) are thorough. The carpenter problem walks through basic algebra from scratch, which is consistent with Part 1's accessibility goal. But it's a jarring tonal break — the reader goes from philosophy to a basic algebra tutorial. The shift is disorienting. The metaphorical problem (2.6.2) is the more interesting application — it maps the math onto life decisions — but it gets less space than the carpenter problem. The priorities are backwards.

## Claims and Explanations

The **intelligence definition** — "the longer you sustain it, the more intelligent you are" — is genuinely problematic. It equates duration with intelligence, and that equation doesn't hold. A monk content for 60 years in a stable monastery isn't smarter than someone who maintained happiness for 10 years while navigating a war zone. The definition conflates durability with difficulty. The framework elsewhere emphasizes that environmental complexity matters, but the definition itself ignores it. This needs fixing, not qualifying.

The **three metaphysical questions** (Why are we here? How are we here? What's our meaning?) are answered in about three sentences total. The speed is dismissive compared to the careful treatment of everything else. A reader who takes these questions seriously — which is most humans who have ever lived — will feel brushed off right before being told "don't get too bored" as the meaning of life.

The **good/evil definition** is strong. Grounding morality in perception and probability without making it absolute is a clean move. The emphasis on "perceived" is important and well-placed.

The **three-step happiness guide** at the end (explore, share, re-explore when bored) is the most actionable takeaway in the chapter. It's practical and memorable. It's also buried as an afterthought after the conclusion, where readers are most likely to skim or stop reading. This is practical gold treated like a footnote. It should be a highlight, not an epilogue.

The **"belief does not entail stupidity, freedom does not entail intelligence"** line is the chapter's best one-liner. It distills the entire 2.5.1 argument into a single sentence — which raises the question of why 2.5.1 needed five paragraphs to say what one sentence handles.

## Overall

The chapter has strong ideas buried under structural problems. The religion section is bloated relative to its function. The intelligence definition has a real flaw (duration is not the same as difficulty). Section 2.5.1 is repetitive. The math tutorial is a jarring tonal break that turns a philosophy chapter into an algebra lesson. And the three-step happiness guide — the chapter's most practical contribution — is tossed in as an afterthought. The F=ma analogy and the fixed variable resolution technique are genuinely good ideas. They deserve a chapter that is built around them, not one that makes the reader wade through religion and repetition to reach them.
