# Part 1: The philosophy of Cheerfulism

---

## 1.- Happiness.

Lots of people say that for a human to have a good life it needs to be happy. But, what is being happy? Before I give you my definition, let's build it from the ground up so it doesn't feel like it fell from the sky.

### 1.1.- Survival comes first.

No fancy re-definitions for now: surviving is staying alive with your basic human needs covered: food, water, sleep and co-existing safely with other humans. You need the co-existing part so you compensate for your individual weaknesses against nature. (a.k.a. it is almost impossible to last alone in the wild)

I will summarize co-existing safely with other humans as, security. This means that just by being alive you do not feel like someone or something can immediately hurt you.

So, so far it is easy, survive is food, water, sleep and security.

This is not controversial. It is biology. You need to be alive to feel anything at all, including happiness. So survival is the first prerequisite, the floor on which everything else gets built.

Now, something interesting happens once that floor is solid. Once survival seems statistically granted, your body does not just sit there content. It does something else entirely.

### 1.2.- Boredom: the engine that drives everything.

A very hard question to answer, or at least it was for me: what is boredom? What is being bored? Well I define being bored as:

> *A state in which your brain does not secrete chemical rewards when you discover, create, analyze, repeat or experience a pattern. A pattern being any activity humans can do: listening to music, playing any game, any sport, an instrument, sex, building, studying... Any activity, even destruction.*

For the rest of the text I will use the terminology "to execute a pattern" as any combination of discovering, creating, analyzing, repeating or experiencing a pattern.

Now, we see that when executing a pattern, eventually you stop secreting chemical rewards. A logical explanation for this would be: This is a mechanism our bodies have so they don't get stuck on a loop doing the same over and over again. Favoring like this exploration and adaptation which are the key factors for avoiding extinction and surviving through time. Boredom is natural, in fact, boredom is essential for a race to adapt and last in time passing through the natural selection filter.

Notice, you can't be bored if you are in danger, if you are hungry, if you are thirsty. Thus boredom only appears when a human is in a safe state. When its basic biological needs are satisfied. It feels natural to understand boredom as a biological mechanism that encourages our bodies to explore when our survival seems statistically granted.

So here is the full definition of boredom as I see it:

> *A biological mechanism humans (and maybe other beings) have which promotes exploration and discovery once their body feels like their survival is statistically granted. Its use resides in promoting the gathering of new information so to try creating a better chance of survival by making humans (and maybe other beings) more prepared against future unexpected changes.*

Notice. Maybe this mechanism is a result of natural selection. Those beings which didn't feel boredom and didn't discover useful information previous to unforeseen events, might have eventually become, or still slowly becoming, extinct.

Boredom is also another key point that forces humans not to live alone. Notice that when loneliness is faced, eventually, as there is a limit on the number of patterns a single human can execute, it will be impossible to escape boredom on your own. When I say loneliness I mean it in its purest sense, not even a dog with you, not a single being apart from yourself. This is another reason why a human needs other beings. Not just for pure biological survival but also to better combat this intrinsic adaptation mechanism we have, called boredom.

This is important. Boredom is not a flaw. It is not a sign that something is wrong with you. It is the engine that drives all exploration, all discovery, all progress. Without boredom we would still be in caves, content with the same four walls. Boredom made us leave.

Notice too that maybe we are not unique in this. Other biological systems might have the same reactions, or even, who knows, non-biological systems have them too? These details will be explored far later in this book because they are not core to this philosophy. Let's for now just go back to humans as it is more useful for us, it is what we are.

### 1.3.- Patterns: the substance of life beyond survival.

Everything humans do, every activity, every thought, every interaction, is a pattern. Cooking is a pattern. Reading is a pattern. Having a conversation is a pattern. Building a house, playing a song, solving a math problem, arguing with your partner, all patterns.

Some patterns trigger chemical rewards in your brain. Others don't. The ones that do, you enjoy. The ones that don't, you find boring. And because boredom pushes you forward, you are always looking for patterns that still trigger those rewards, or returning to old ones hoping they will trigger them again.

Executing patterns is how boredom gets addressed. The search for rewarding patterns is, in a very real sense, the substance of a life beyond survival.

### 1.4.- Freedom: a practical thing.

I define freedom as:

> *The capability of satisfying a need or desire in multiple ways.*

Let me briefly clarify something before continuing. I'm not discussing freedom in a deep philosophical way. Some might define freedom as "the ability to have done otherwise." This text isn't about deep existential debates. Instead, it's a practical guide to help find solutions for human needs. Questions like 'to be or not to be' or debates about free will are complex and, for now, without clear answers. I won't delve into them here. My goal is to offer logical insights into human existence and give real-world advice to enhance it.

However, way later in the book, for the ones interested in these deep philosophical topics I will include a section on metaphysics.

Continuing. The freer you are the more options you will have to satisfy your needs.

If someone has a problem and only one way of solving it, chances are that, if that solution path, for whatever reason, becomes impossible or harder, the person will likely not be happy. Thus the more options you get the higher your chances of being happy... or are they?

Only if you are intelligent enough to handle them. More freedom with no intelligence to use it is just more ways to get lost. I will develop this more in depth in the next chapter.

Notice, you can be happy without freedom or just with less freedom as long as your survival and boredom needs are satisfied. Freedom is not the goal. It is a means. This distinction will become very important later in the book when we talk about how societies organize resources.

### 1.5.- The definition of happiness.

Now we have all the pieces. Survival is the floor. Boredom is the engine. Patterns are the substance. Freedom is the range of options. Intelligence is the capacity to handle those options. Put them together and here is what I think happiness is:

> *Happiness is what a being feels when survival is perceived to be statistically granted and possesses enough intelligence and strength to avoid boredom on a desired basis.*

This definition arises from 2 facts:

1. You need to be alive to feel happiness, thus the surviving part of the definition.

2. If you get bored for too long you won't feel what we call happiness. Notice, different humans have different boredom tolerances. However, all of them have a tolerance threshold from which, if surpassed, they will start suffering.

### 1.6.- The factors: health, money, relationships.

Now, all this being defined, we can observe that the next factor for feeling happiness is being entertained, which is just not being bored. The frequency and the variety of activities vary on an individual basis. So the quantity of the following requisites you will require will be different for each one of you.

This is the most complex part of mastering happiness. It can only be done sustainably via self analysis and introspection. You will have to figure out how to get the following requisites and how much of each so as to satisfy your unique needs:

- **Health:** Enough health, physical and mental, to carry out the activities that you enjoy. The healthier the more activities you can carry out for longer. Thus the more solutions you will have to the boredom problem. Thus the more likely you are to be happy. But this is just statistics. On an individual level you decide how much health you really need. A clear example of this is: An obese climber will be less likely (or will find it harder) to enjoy the views at the summit of a mountain. Due to their obesity. So, someone whose happiness is brought by climbing, should stay in moderate weight to get a better chance of executing that pattern (that activity) that he enjoys.

- **Money:** A tool for freedom, not the end goal. Money does not buy happiness, but buys other people's time so you can spend yours freely. And as mentioned in the health part, the freer you are the more activities you can do to satisfy your boredom. Not everyone needs to be rich in order to not suffer from boredom, some people will need more, others less. You should analyze how much money is usually required to carry out the activities you like and whether you can afford them long term so you can live a long happy life.

- **Relationships with other beings:** As mentioned some paragraphs ago, a human alone will eventually get bored. Relations with other beings are needed, an animal, another human etc. These add randomness to the interactions resulting in unpredictable patterns to execute. Patterns which will likely secrete the proper happiness chemicals in your brain as they are something new, something being explored.

All these factors are easy to name yet harder to analyze for each individual. That is why chapter 3 of this book delves deeper into the intricacies of analyzing complex problems. It also talks about how to analyze them in the most efficient way possible given our current capabilities. I wrote that chapter so anyone can give their best effort to try and answer for themselves the questions presented.

I'm mentioning chapter 3 and we are not even in chapter 2. Let's not get too much ahead of ourselves. But before moving on, there is one more concept I need to introduce here because it ties the whole happiness model together.

### 1.7.- "More Enough": not everyone needs the same amount.

I said that health, money, and freedom matter for happiness. But how much of each? This is where most people, and most political systems, get it wrong. They assume either that everyone needs the same amount, or that more is always better.

Neither is true.

Think about it this way. There are people who need very little money to be happy. A simple life, a few hobbies, a small circle of friends, and they are genuinely content. There are others who need more because their patterns, the activities that kill their boredom, cost more. A person whose happiness comes from sailing needs more money than a person whose happiness comes from hiking. Neither is wrong. Neither is more enlightened. They just have different boredom profiles.

The same applies to health, to freedom, to relationships, to everything. Each person has a level of each that is enough for their happiness. Not the maximum. Not the average. Their enough.

I call this concept "More Enough." It means: the amount of something that is sufficient to sustain your happiness, given your unique pattern needs and boredom tolerance.

The reason this matters is that most of the conflict in the world, most of the political arguments about how to distribute resources, how much freedom people should have, what the State should provide and what it shouldn't, all of these implicitly assume a universal answer to "how much is enough?" And there isn't one. There are billions of individual answers.

"More Enough" is the antidote to two extremes. On one side, asceticism, the idea that wanting less is always morally superior. On the other, consumerism, the idea that wanting more is always the path to happiness. Both are wrong because both ignore the individual. Some people need more. Some people need less. The intelligent move is to figure out your own enough, not to copy someone else's.

A cheap car that gets you to the mountains you love to hike is more enough for someone whose patterns are about nature. A Ferrari is not more. It is just extra variables to handle for no added happiness. But for someone whose patterns involve automotive engineering, the Ferrari might genuinely be their enough. The point is not to judge the amount but to understand the function it serves for the individual.

This concept will come back throughout the book. When we talk about how societies distribute resources, when we talk about freedom versus happiness as a guiding principle, when we talk about what world peace actually requires. For now, just remember: enough is personal, and figuring out your own enough is one of the most intelligent things you can do.

### 1.8.- The Four Pillars of Happiness.

Summing up everything so far, we can understand happiness as standing on four pillars. Two are visible and obvious. Two are hidden and easy to miss.

**The visible pillars:**

1. **Survival.** Food, water, sleep, security. The biological floor.
2. **Entertainment.** Enough pattern execution to avoid sustained boredom. The substance of life beyond the floor.

**The hidden pillars:**

3. **Freedom.** Enough options to satisfy your needs in multiple ways, so that if one path closes, others remain open.
4. **Intelligence.** The capacity to handle those options, to analyze your situation, to figure out what your enough is, and to sustain your happiness over time.

Without survival, nothing else matters. Without entertainment, you suffer even in safety. Without freedom, you are fragile because one blocked path can break you. Without intelligence, all the freedom in the world is just noise you can't navigate.

These four pillars interact. Health expands your pattern options, so it serves both entertainment and freedom. Money buys other people's time, so it serves freedom. Relationships add randomness and new patterns, so they serve entertainment. But health, money, and relationships are not pillars themselves, they are factors that feed into the four.

Thus we can see and read that happiness is an effective biological mechanism for evolution. Which means it is used in nature to survive and thrive as a species over time. ### Conclusion: Chapter 1.

This has been chapter 1. You now have the model:

- Survival is the floor. Without it, nothing.
- Boredom is the engine. It drives exploration, discovery, progress.
- Patterns are everything you do. Executing them is the substance of life.
- Freedom is how many ways you can satisfy a need.
- Intelligence is the capacity to handle those ways.
- Happiness is what you feel when survival is handled and you have enough intelligence and freedom to avoid boredom on your terms.
- "More Enough" is the recognition that how much of anything you need is personal.
- The Four Pillars, survival, entertainment, freedom, and intelligence, are the structure that holds it all.

Important is to know that each person has a unique need of pattern execution and boredom tolerance. Figuring out yours is an act of intelligence. Sustaining it over time is the definition of being intelligent.

Welcome to the beginning of Cheerfulism.
