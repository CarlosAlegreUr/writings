# Critique: Chapter 10 — About understanding metaphysics. Is there anything real? Is there anything not real?

## Pacing and Structure

This chapter tries to dismiss all of metaphysics in 95 lines. That is intellectually lazy. Thousands of serious thinkers across millennia have engaged with metaphysics productively — in physics, logic, computer science, philosophy of mind. You do not get to wave away an entire discipline in fewer words than a restaurant menu just because one particular loop frustrates you.

The structure is: framing (metaphysics is useless) -> anecdote (friends discussing religion in Barcelona) -> demonstration (the self-reference loop) -> mantra ("rocks can rock") -> conclusion. The arc has internal logic and the pacing is fine for the length. That is not the problem. The problem is the length itself and what it signals about how seriously the author took the question.

**The opening framing** (lines 1-15) states the same position three times in slightly different words before the anecdote starts. Pick one and move on. The buried observation that metaphysics is "a great sign" for a society — meaning they are surviving well enough to think abstractly — is genuinely interesting and deserves its own moment instead of being lost in repetitive throat-clearing.

**The anecdote** (section 10.1) is the chapter's best section and it is not close. The scene is vivid: public pianos, Barcelona, a Christian, a Muslim, a Rastafari, and the author. The conversation flows naturally. The Cristobal Colon exchange is sharp — radical epistemological skepticism delivered in everyday language. This grounds what could be a purely abstract chapter in a real human moment. More of this, less of the posturing.

**The self-reference loop demonstration** (section 10.1.1) is the chapter's biggest creative risk. Instead of explaining why metaphysics is circular, the text performs it — spiraling, contradicting, trailing off, leaving blanks. This is genuinely clever. But here is the thing: it proves that the author finds metaphysics frustrating, not that metaphysics is useless. Frustration is not a philosophical argument. The demonstration shows a cognitive wall; it does not prove there is nothing on the other side. The section also runs too long for a single trick. The reader gets the point by the second spiral. By the fourth, they are skimming. And announcing the trick beforehand ("Okay prepare your logical reasoning") kills the surprise.

**The rocks mantra** is memorable and placed well. Appears twice — body and conclusion — which gives the chapter a clean frame. The Kung Fu Panda quote that follows is tonally lighter and undercuts the mantra. You had a good exit line and then kept talking.

**The conclusion** and **transition to Chapter 11** both work. The transition does genuine structural work: explains the next chapter's origin, introduces the Sapiens connection, credits the friend who prompted it.

## Claims and Explanations

The chapter makes one claim: metaphysics is a cognitive loop that humans cannot resolve, so Cheerfulism avoids it. The claim is stated clearly. The demonstration is effective as an experience. But the argument has a serious problem.

The chapter equates all of metaphysics with the first-cause loop. This is like equating all of mathematics with "what is infinity?" Metaphysics as a discipline covers the nature of time, causation, identity, possibility, the structure of reality — questions that have produced practically useful insights across multiple fields. The chapter's dismissal works against the specific loop it demonstrates. As a blanket rejection of the entire field, it is indefensible. Anyone with a philosophy background will see this immediately.

The epistemological claim in the anecdote — that we cannot truly know whether Columbus discovered America — is provocative but sloppy. There is a vast difference between "we cannot have absolute certainty about anything" and "we have no good reason to believe historical claims." The chapter collapses these two positions as if they are the same. They are not.

And then there is the elephant: "Fuck metaphysics and let's eat tasty food together" is anti-intellectual. This is a strange posture for a book that champions intelligence and curiosity. You cannot spend chapters arguing that intelligence is a core human value and then dismiss an entire field of inquiry because it made your head hurt. The casual dismissal contradicts the book's own principles.

## Overall

Chapter 10 is structurally cohesive and the Barcelona anecdote is genuinely good writing. The performative loop demonstration is clever as a device but proves frustration, not uselessness. The fundamental problem is that dismissing all of metaphysics in 95 lines — and doing it with a shrug and a food reference — is not the move of a thinker taking ideas seriously. It is the move of someone who hit a hard problem and decided to mock it instead of sitting with it. The chapter's definition of metaphysics is so narrow that its dismissal does not land for anyone who knows what the field actually contains. The anecdote deserves a better chapter around it.
