# Critique: Chapter 14 — Acknowledgments

## Pacing and Structure

Roughly 17 lines. The arc: general thanks to free knowledge sharers, two specific names (Benjamin Keep and Justin Sung), a lengthy caveat about how little the author knows about them, a broadening to all historical contributors, and a closing call to self-improvement.

The caveat paragraph is the longest passage and it is a problem. The author thanks two people by name and then admits he watched only three of their videos, cannot vouch for their reliability, and does not even remember their faces. This defeats the purpose of an acknowledgment. Thanking someone and then saying "I don't remember a single thing from those videos, not even the faces" is not radical honesty — it is insulting dressed up as transparency. Either these people mattered enough to name or they did not. Pick one.

## Claims and Explanations

The observation that sharing free knowledge is "a form of empathy" is consistent with the book's framework. The broader civilizational gratitude — "Millions have lived and died, suffered and enjoyed in order for this text to become a reality" — is the section's strongest move. It widens scope from individual thanks to something genuinely felt, and it saves the section from being just the awkward YouTuber paragraph.

## Overall

The civilizational gratitude pivot saves this chapter. Without it, the acknowledgments would be an author naming people he cannot remember to thank them for things he does not recall. The broader framing lands well and feels consistent with the book's voice. The YouTuber caveat still needs to be either cut or rewritten — as it stands, it undermines the very act of acknowledgment.
