# Critique: Chapter 4 — Thoughts on the ideal society, part 1: On power dynamics

## Pacing and Structure

This chapter tries to be five chapters and none of them get adequate treatment. It attempts to cover the origins of power, ethics/morality definitions, the static-dynamic dilemma, democracy vs anarchism, graph theory, information management and politics, emotions, nationalism, a novel law-adaptation proposal, army redistribution, and the prisoners' dilemma. That's not ambition — it's a lack of editing.

**The opening (pre-4.1)** is one of the strongest introductions in the book. The conflict, talk or fight, strength, coordination chain is clear and builds naturally. The f(m,y) = m * (1 + y) power function is a neat formalization — simple enough to follow and concretely demonstrates the muscle-vs-information tradeoff. The numerical example makes the point land. The disclaimer about the math not being physics-precise is honest and well-placed.

**Section 4.1.1 (ethics/morality)** is bloated. It takes roughly 40 lines to clarify definitions (moral vs ethics vs judgment) that could be done in 15. The core insight is simple: ethics = the whole labeling process, morality = the guiding principles, judgment = the application. That's three sentences. The "Antonio" naming device is entertaining, but the section spends too long clarifying common definitions and explaining why they're confusing. The reader wants to get to the static-dynamic dilemma. This section stands in the way.

**Section 4.1.1 proper (the static-dynamic dilemma)** is the chapter's most important idea and its best-argued one. The Bob/Alice tractor example is concrete and immediately relatable. The One Piece quote from Doflamingo is a surprisingly effective choice — it says in dramatic fiction what the preceding analysis says in analytical language. The connection back to the fixed variable resolution technique (static ethics = fixed variables, dynamic ethics = varying ones) is the payoff of three chapters of setup, and it lands.

**Section 4.1.2 (democracy and anarchism)** is where the chapter starts to feel overloaded. The argument that democracy coordinates the static-dynamic dilemma is stated clearly. The anarchism analysis reads as exploratory rather than conclusive — the author openly says "more on this later." This section raises more questions than it answers, which creates deferral, not progress.

**Section 4.1.2.1 (graph theory)** is one of the most concrete and well-constructed arguments in the entire book. Building up from 1 person to 5, showing how relations grow non-linearly, connecting this to the impossibility of anarchism at scale — this is the engineer's method from chapter 3 applied perfectly. The leap from graph complexity to uncertainty to stress to violence to State formation is a genuinely original chain of reasoning. The chapter at its analytical best.

**Section 4.1.3 (information management)** makes its point efficiently: religion was the original mass-communication technology, now replaced by internet/AI/blockchain. The "free market of dichotomies" observation — that the supply of opposing narratives increased while demand stayed the same, devaluing religion — is an original economic metaphor that works.

**Section 4.1.3.1 (emotions)** is compact and effective. Defining emotions as "fixing lots of variables at once" is consistent with the book's framework and offers real explanatory power. The predator/log example is vivid. The key claim — that we haven't evolved for modern complexity — is well-supported.

**Section 4.1.4 (recap and dichotomies)** runs long. The three examples of modern dichotomies (feminism, ecology, sexuality) are concrete. But the analysis of politicians caught in vicious cycles goes on for roughly 80 lines restating the same observation about simplification and polarization. The three-reason breakdown (economic dependence, fear, laziness) is clear. The surrounding text says the same thing from five different angles. This section needs compression, not more angles.

**Section 4.1.5 (nationalism)** is a chapter crammed into a section. The core proposal — nationalism based on law and language rather than culture and tradition — is a clean thesis. The bullfighting example is the perfect illustration. But the section then branches into how Cheerful individuals will change borders, an aside on language evolution, and the progressive asymmetrical statistical gauge of law proposal. Each of these is interesting. None of them get enough space. This is what happens when a chapter-sized idea is forced into a section.

**The "progressive asymmetrical statistical gauge of law" (4.1.5.4)** is one of the most original ideas in the entire book. The asymmetry principle — it should take longer to earn leniency than to trigger strictness — maps onto an intuitive truth about human behavior. The two examples (crime rates, sugar tax) make the mechanism concrete. And it's buried in a sub-sub-subsection of nationalism. That's criminal waste of a good idea. This deserves prominent placement, not a nesting depth that most readers won't reach.

**Section 4.2 (Cheerfulism and new tech)** is the conclusion in disguise. The prisoners' dilemma application to military spending (4.2.3.1) is a neat analogy but arrives very late in a very long chapter, when the reader's stamina is gone. The personal disclaimer (line 769: "I'm not specialized in politics... this is just a hobby in my young life") is honest — but it also raises the obvious question: if this is just a hobby, then why 800 lines of political theory? Either commit to the analysis or don't write 800 lines of it. The disclaimer undercuts the chapter it's trying to protect.

The **transition** is effective. The reiteration that Cheerfulism does not equal the author's political conclusions continues the important structural move from chapter 3. The personal quote about looking at the past vs the future is a clean closer.

## Claims and Explanations

The **power function f(m,y) = m * (1 + y)** is presented as a model, not a law, and the disclaimer is clear. One mathematical note: when y is 0 (no information management), power equals m — raw headcount. The function implies that a completely uncoordinated group still has power equal to its size. An uncoordinated mob might have less effective power than its headcount suggests. Minor quibble since the author explicitly says the math is approximate.

The **graph theory argument for why anarchism fails at scale** is the chapter's strongest analytical contribution. Relations grow as n(n-1)/2, humans have finite cognitive capacity, therefore beyond some group size, full mutual understanding is impossible. This is essentially Dunbar's number derived from first principles, though the author doesn't reference Dunbar. Whether deliberate omission or independent derivation, the argument stands on its own.

The **"State as the winning mafia" framing** is provocative but immediately qualified (the author admits his libertarian tendencies are showing). This self-awareness is good practice. The qualification that States can also emerge from peaceful agreement prevents the argument from collapsing into pure libertarian ideology.

The **nationalism-as-law-and-language thesis** is the chapter's boldest original claim. It has an unaddressed tension: the author says language evolves naturally, but language evolution can also create fractures — dialects becoming separate languages, minority languages being suppressed. The Catalan/Basque situation in Spain, which the author would know firsthand, is a natural test case that isn't discussed. The thesis would be stronger if it acknowledged that language, like culture, can be a source of nationalist conflict, not just a neutral coordination tool.

The **emotions-as-variable-fixing** framework is one of the book's most useful contributions. The claim that emotions are useful in evolutionarily familiar contexts but dangerous in novel complex ones is consistent with mainstream evolutionary psychology, though no sources are cited.

The **vicious cycle of politicians and simplified truths** (section 4.1.4) is well-observed but stated as description rather than argument. The dynamic described (simplification to polarization to alienation to more simplification) applies more to populist democracies than to technocratic or authoritarian systems. This doesn't invalidate the point but narrows its scope more than the chapter acknowledges.

## Overall

Chapter 4 is the book's most ambitious chapter and its most undisciplined one. It tries to cover five chapters' worth of material in one, and the result is that genuinely original ideas — the graph theory argument, the progressive asymmetrical statistical gauge of law, the emotions-as-variable-fixing framework — are buried under bloated definitions, repetitive political analysis, and a nationalism section that needed to be its own chapter. The ethics definitions take 40 lines for a distinction that needs 15. The law-adaptation proposal, one of the most original ideas in the book, is hidden in a sub-sub-subsection. The "this is just a hobby" disclaimer is honest but sits awkwardly next to 800 lines of political theory that suggest otherwise. The chapter needs to be split or aggressively cut. The good ideas deserve better than the structure they're trapped in.
