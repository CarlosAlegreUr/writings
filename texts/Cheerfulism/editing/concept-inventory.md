## Part 1: The Philosophy of Cheerfulism (Chapters 0-3)

### 1. Cheerfulism
- **One-line definition:** A practical philosophy that says "maximize your intelligence to pursue your optimal happiness," built on scientific understanding of human nature.
- **Current location(s):** Ch0, Ch3 (section 3.5), referenced throughout all chapters.
- **Dependency:** Happiness (Ch1), Intelligence (Ch2), Fixed Variable Resolution Technique (Ch2-3).
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Aristotelian eudaimonia combined with Enlightenment rationalism; echoes of pragmatism (William James, John Dewey).

### 2. Happiness (definition)
- **One-line definition:** What a being feels when survival is perceived to be statistically granted and it possesses enough intelligence and strength to avoid boredom on a desired basis.
- **Current location(s):** Ch1 (section 1.0).
- **Dependency:** None (foundational).
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Aristotle's eudaimonia; Epicurean hedonic calculus; Maslow's hierarchy of needs (self-actualization after basic needs).

### 3. Survival (as prerequisite to happiness)
- **One-line definition:** Staying alive with basic human needs covered -- food, water, sleep, and security (co-existing safely with others).
- **Current location(s):** Ch1 (section 1.1).
- **Dependency:** None (foundational).
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Maslow's physiological and safety needs.

### 4. Boredom (biological mechanism)
- **One-line definition:** A biological mechanism that promotes exploration and discovery once survival is statistically granted, driving pattern-seeking to improve future adaptability.
- **Current location(s):** Ch1 (section 1.1).
- **Dependency:** Survival, Happiness.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Heidegger's "profound boredom" as existential motivator; evolutionary psychology's exploration drive.

### 5. Pattern / Pattern Execution
- **One-line definition:** Any activity humans can do (discovering, creating, analyzing, repeating, or experiencing), which when executed may or may not trigger chemical happiness rewards.
- **Current location(s):** Ch1 (section 1.1), referenced throughout.
- **Dependency:** Boredom, Happiness.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Behavioral psychology's reinforcement patterns; Csikszentmihalyi's flow states.

### 6. Freedom (definition)
- **One-line definition:** The capability of satisfying a need or desire in multiple ways -- explicitly practical, not metaphysical.
- **Current location(s):** Ch1 (section 1.2).
- **Dependency:** Happiness.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Isaiah Berlin's positive liberty; John Stuart Mill's liberty as capability.

### 7. The Four Pillars of Happiness
- **One-line definition:** Happiness rests on two visible pillars (survival and entertainment) and two hidden pillars (freedom and intelligence).
- **Current location(s):** Ch1 (conclusion).
- **Dependency:** Happiness, Freedom, Intelligence.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Maslow's hierarchy restructured as a four-factor model.

### 8. Health (as happiness factor)
- **One-line definition:** Enough physical and mental health to carry out the activities you enjoy; more health means more options to combat boredom.
- **Current location(s):** Ch1 (section 1.1), Ch12 (FAQ on why health is not a core pillar).
- **Dependency:** Happiness, Freedom.
- **Status:** Supporting
- **Closest historical analog:** WHO definition of health as "complete physical, mental and social well-being."

### 9. Money (as happiness factor)
- **One-line definition:** A tool for freedom, not the end goal; it buys other people's time so you can spend yours freely.
- **Current location(s):** Ch1 (section 1.1).
- **Dependency:** Freedom, Happiness.
- **Status:** Supporting
- **Closest historical analog:** Aristotle's chrematistics; modern "money buys options" economic psychology.

### 10. Relationships (as happiness factor)
- **One-line definition:** Connections with other beings that add randomness and new patterns to execute, needed for both survival and entertainment.
- **Current location(s):** Ch1 (section 1.1).
- **Dependency:** Survival, Boredom.
- **Status:** Supporting
- **Closest historical analog:** Aristotle's emphasis on friendship (philia); attachment theory.

### 11. Intelligence (definition)
- **One-line definition:** The ability to handle patterns in such a way that uses work to reach some goal along time; the longer the time it is maintained for, the more intelligence is needed.
- **Current location(s):** Ch2 (section 2.5).
- **Dependency:** Happiness, Patterns.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Howard Gardner's multiple intelligences; general factor 'g' reframed as goal-sustaining capacity.

### 12. Human Intelligence (definition)
- **One-line definition:** The ability to handle patterns in such a way that maintains survival and later happiness along time.
- **Current location(s):** Ch2 (section 2.5).
- **Dependency:** Intelligence, Happiness, Survival.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Sternberg's practical intelligence; evolutionary fitness.

### 13. Fixed Variable Resolution Technique
- **One-line definition:** The natural brain process of fixing some variables (beliefs/assumptions) to solve for others, then revisiting the fixed ones -- the core of all rational problem-solving.
- **Current location(s):** Ch2 (section 2.1, 2.2, 2.6), Ch3, referenced in Ch4-8, Ch11-12.
- **Dependency:** None (foundational method).
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Cartesian method of doubt; scientific method's hypothesis testing; ceteris paribus in economics.

### 14. Belief (definition)
- **One-line definition:** A fixed rule a human follows that hardly ever changes because it is never questioned or doubted upon -- the "fixed" part of the fixed variable resolution technique.
- **Current location(s):** Ch2 (section 2.2, 2.3).
- **Dependency:** Fixed Variable Resolution Technique.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Pragmatist epistemology (Peirce's fixation of belief); Kuhn's paradigms.

### 15. Good and Evil (definition)
- **One-line definition:** Labels for things perceived to grant better or worse likelihood of survival and happiness according to an individual or group's personalities and their surrounding nature.
- **Current location(s):** Ch2 (section 2.3.1.1), Ch8 (section 8.1.2.1).
- **Dependency:** Happiness, Survival.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Moral relativism; Nietzsche's "beyond good and evil"; utilitarian consequentialism.

### 16. Religion (analysis as belief system)
- **One-line definition:** A manifestation of fixed beliefs that provides meaning, community, and hope -- useful but limiting due to slow adaptability.
- **Current location(s):** Ch2 (section 2.3).
- **Dependency:** Belief, Happiness, Boredom.
- **Status:** Supporting
- **Closest historical analog:** Durkheim's functional analysis of religion; Marx's "opium of the people."

### 17. Three Benefits of Religion
- **One-line definition:** (1) Gives meaning to life, (2) satisfies community belonging need, (3) provides hope in positions of weakness.
- **Current location(s):** Ch2 (sections 2.3.1-2.3.3).
- **Dependency:** Religion, Happiness.
- **Status:** Supporting
- **Closest historical analog:** Durkheim's sacred/profane distinction; Victor Frankl's logotherapy (meaning).

### 18. Rationality and Faith as Two Faces of the Same Coin
- **One-line definition:** Both rational thinking (variable) and faith/belief (fixed) are complementary components of the human problem-solving technique.
- **Current location(s):** Ch2 (section 2.4).
- **Dependency:** Belief, Fixed Variable Resolution Technique.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Kierkegaard's leap of faith alongside reason; Peirce's inquiry model.

### 19. Thinking Like an Engineer / The Engineers' Way of Thinking
- **One-line definition:** A 10-step method for analyzing any complex system by defining, modularizing, researching, going back-and-forth, and consolidating learning.
- **Current location(s):** Ch3 (sections 3.1-3.2), referenced in Ch7, Ch8, Ch11.
- **Dependency:** Fixed Variable Resolution Technique.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Systems thinking (Bertalanffy); design thinking; scientific method formalized as iterative analysis.

### 20. The 10 Steps of Thinking Like an Engineer
- **One-line definition:** Choose a system, define it, state your goal, divide into sub-modules, analyze modules, redefine if needed, note insights, repeat for other modules, iterate until done, consolidate.
- **Current location(s):** Ch3 (section 3.1).
- **Dependency:** Thinking Like an Engineer.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Polya's "How to Solve It" heuristics; PDCA cycle (Deming).

### 21. Progressive Overload (applied to thinking)
- **One-line definition:** The principle from physical training applied to intellectual development -- start with simple topics and progressively increase complexity.
- **Current location(s):** Ch3 (introduction).
- **Dependency:** Thinking Like an Engineer.
- **Status:** Supporting
- **Closest historical analog:** Vygotsky's zone of proximal development; scaffolding in education.

### 22. Three Lines of Action (of Cheerfulism)
- **One-line definition:** (1) Always learn and question when in discomfort. (2) Explore as response to discomfort, sometimes alone, sometimes with people. (3) Be peaceful but able to defend yourself; use violence only in imminent life-or-death situations.
- **Current location(s):** Ch3 (section 3.5).
- **Dependency:** Cheerfulism.
- **Status:** Core
- **Closest historical analog:** Stoic praxis; Buddhist middle way; Gandhian non-violence with self-defense exception.

### 23. Cheerful (as identity)
- **One-line definition:** A person who faces life with the philosophy of Cheerfulism -- a self-identifier for practitioners.
- **Current location(s):** Ch3 (section 3.5, point 2).
- **Dependency:** Cheerfulism.
- **Status:** Supporting
- **Closest historical analog:** "Stoic" as both philosophy and identity label; "Epicurean."

## Part 2: A Cheerful Understanding of Human Societies (Chapters 4-8)

- **Closest historical analog:** Machiavelli's realpolitik; Weber's theory of power and domination.
